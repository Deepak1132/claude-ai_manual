Chapter 01 · What is Claude

What is Claude?

You have probably heard of AI chatbots. Claude is different. It is not built to answer frequently asked questions or route support tickets. It is built to think alongside you — to reason through hard problems, write with clarity, research across documents, and collaborate on complex professional work. This chapter explains exactly what Claude is, how it is designed, and why that design matters.

After this chapter you will be able to

Core Concept — What Makes Claude Different

Claude is created by Anthropic, an AI safety company. What sets Claude apart is the framework behind every response: Constitutional AI. This is a training method that teaches Claude to align with human values — to be genuinely helpful, to avoid causing harm, and to be honest even when the truth is less convenient than a confident-sounding answer.

Early users consistently report that Claude is more steerable — you can direct its tone, style, and focus — and less likely to produce harmful or misleading outputs. That reliability is the foundation that makes Claude useful for serious professional work.

Chapter 03 · Writing Effective Prompts

Writing Effective Prompts

Every interaction with Claude starts with a prompt. A stronger prompt produces a stronger result — consistently, every time. This chapter teaches the three-part framework used by effective Claude users: Setting the Stage, Defining the Task, and Specifying Rules. Master this structure and you will get better outputs from your very first message.

After this chapter you will be able to
  • Write effective prompts using the Stage → Task → Rules framework
  • Use file uploads to give Claude richer context
  • Iterate on Claude's responses with specific, targeted feedback
  • Troubleshoot the most common prompting mistakes
 Video — Getting started with Claude.ai

Core Concept — The Three-Part Prompt Framework

Think of Claude as a highly capable colleague who has joined your team today. They are brilliant — but they don't know your context, your audience, your tone preferences, or your deadlines. The more clearly you brief them, the better their work will be.

Before writing any prompt, think through three elements:

Visual — The Prompt Workflow Diagram
How a strong prompt flows to a strong response
1 · User Context (Setting the Stage)
↓
2 · Task Definition (Defining the Task)
↓
3 · Rules & Output Format (Specifying Rules)
↓
Claude Response
Step 01 — Stage

Set the Context

Who are you? What is your role? What background does Claude need to respond appropriately? Without context, Claude defaults to generic — which is rarely what you need.

Step 02 — Task

Define the Action

What exactly do you want Claude to do? Write, analyze, build, compare, summarize, debug? The more specific the task, the more useful the result.

Step 03 — Rules

Specify the Output

What tone? What length? What format — bullet points, a report, a table, under 200 words? Provide examples of ideal outputs where you have them.

Example — Framework Applied to Market Research

Real-World Scenario

A marketing lead preparing an investor pitch

Without the framework: "Tell me about the streaming market." Claude produces a generic Wikipedia-level overview.

With the framework: role established (marketing lead at an indie streaming startup preparing a pitch deck), task defined (research market trends, competitor positioning, growth opportunities), rules specified (current web research, citations, professional report structure). Claude produces a targeted, formatted, cited research brief that goes directly into the deck.

Prompt Example

Prompt · Market Research for Investor Pitch
I'm the marketing lead at an indie streaming startup preparing an investor pitch deck. Research the current state of the independent film streaming market. Identify: key trends, competitor positioning, and growth opportunities. Use current web research with citations. Structure the findings as a professional report with an executive summary at the top.

What this prompt does:

Produces a targeted, cited market research report formatted for direct use in a pitch deck — not a generic overview.

Why this prompt works:

  • Setting the stage: "marketing lead at an indie streaming startup preparing an investor pitch deck" — role and purpose in one sentence
  • Defining the task: three specific research areas prevent Claude from wandering into irrelevant territory
  • Specifying rules: web research with citations and professional report structure are stated explicitly

Common Prompting Problems & Fixes

ProblemRoot CauseFix
Response is too genericInsufficient contextAdd your role, audience, and specific constraints in Stage
Wrong lengthLength not specifiedState it directly: "two paragraphs" or "under 100 words"
Wrong formatFormat not specifiedDescribe the structure or paste an example of what you want
Wrong toneClaude defaults to professionalDescribe tone plainly: "casual," "formal," "like talking to a friend"
Incorrect factsClaude can hallucinate niche detailsAsk Claude to cite sources; use web search for verification
★
Key Takeaways
  • Every strong prompt has three parts: Stage (context), Task (action), Rules (format and style)
  • Speak to Claude like a knowledgeable new colleague — naturally, specifically, with full context
  • File uploads give Claude rich context without re-explaining documents each time
  • Iteration is the real skill: treat every first response as a draft, not a final deliverable

Chapter 04 · Claude Desktop Modes

Claude Desktop Modes

The Claude desktop app offers three distinct modes — Chat, Cowork, and Code — each designed for a different type of work. Understanding which mode to use is the difference between a quick answer and a fully completed complex project.

After this chapter you will be able to
  • Identify the three modes and what each is optimized for
  • Explain the unique capabilities of each mode
  • Match any task to the right mode immediately

Core Concept — Three Modes, Three Use Cases

Visual — Mode Comparison
ModeOptimized ForKey Additions
💬 ChatQuick exchanges, brainstorming, iterative drafting, back-and-forth dialogueQuick entry overlay (Option key), screenshots, dictation, desktop connectors
🗂️ CoworkComplex sustained work: research briefs, cross-source analysis, polished deliverablesFolder access, scheduled tasks, browser use, plugins, simultaneous subtasks
⌨️ CodeSoftware development: write, test, run, deploy, refactor codeVisual diffs, git integration, Ask / Code / Plan sub-modes, local + remote environments

Chat Mode — Fast, Contextual, Always Accessible

Chat is the same Claude experience you know from claude.ai, with native desktop additions that reduce friction while you're working in other apps:

  • Quick entry: Double-tap the Option key to pull Claude up as a floating overlay — without leaving your current app
  • Screenshots and window sharing: Claude sees exactly what you're looking at — no manual description needed
  • Dictation: Talk through a problem; Claude drafts from what you say
  • Desktop connectors: Connect local tools and services so Claude can access apps on your machine
💡

Power move: Set up a morning routine prompt in Chat mode — paste it in as you make coffee and Claude drafts your daily priorities from your calendar and inbox while you're away.

Cowork Mode — Complex, Multi-Source, Sustained Work

Cowork is built for work that requires pulling from many sources, synthesizing, and producing something polished. Claude can run multiple subtasks in parallel — reading files, browsing the web, checking integrations — and bring the results together into one deliverable.

🔒

Protected environment: Cowork runs in a contained environment on your computer. Claude reads, creates, and edits files only within the folders you explicitly share — nothing else is accessible.

Code Mode — Full Development Environment

Code mode is a complete development environment inside the desktop app, powered by Claude Code. Claude reads your codebase, writes and modifies code, runs commands, and tracks every change with git. You choose how much autonomy to give Claude:

  • Ask: Claude proposes every change and waits for your approval before modifying anything
  • Code: Claude applies file changes automatically but checks in before running terminal commands
  • Plan: Claude outlines its full approach before touching anything — review the plan, then execute

Example — Choosing the Right Mode

Real-World Scenario

Three tasks, three modes, same afternoon

2:00 PM — Quick question: A colleague sends a Slack message about a dashboard. The PM double-taps Option, shares the dashboard screenshot, and asks Claude in Chat mode what the key metric to focus on is. Answer in 30 seconds.

2:30 PM — Research brief: The PM opens Cowork mode, asks Claude to research the top five competitors and compile a brief from their websites, G2 reviews, and the company's own market research docs in Google Drive. Claude runs the searches in parallel and delivers the brief.

4:00 PM — Bug fix: The developer opens Code mode (Plan sub-mode), describes the bug, reviews Claude's plan, approves it, and watches the fix applied with a visual diff. Done before end of day.

Prompt Example

Prompt · Cowork Mode — Competitive Research Brief
I'm a VP of Product evaluating project management tools for our 80-person engineering org. Research the top 5 enterprise project management tools: Asana, Monday.com, ClickUp, Linear, Notion. For each tool, find: 1. Enterprise pricing (current) 2. Most-praised features (from G2 or Capterra reviews) 3. Most common customer complaints 4. Major product updates in the last 6 months Deliver a structured report with a summary comparison table at the top. Cite all sources.

What this prompt does:

Produces a structured, cited competitive analysis across five tools from multiple sources — a task that would take 2–3 hours manually.

Why Cowork mode:

  • Requires visiting multiple websites and review platforms simultaneously
  • Cross-references and synthesizes findings into a single formatted report
  • Cowork's browser access and parallel subtasks handle all of this automatically
★
Key Takeaways
  • Chat = fast exchanges, enhanced with Quick Entry overlay, screenshots, and dictation
  • Cowork = complex, multi-source deliverables with browser access and scheduled tasks
  • Code = full development environment with visual diffs, git, and three autonomy levels
  • Cowork and Code both run on Claude Code under the hood — same engine, different scope

Chapter 05 · Projects

Projects

Without Projects, every new Claude conversation starts from zero. You re-upload the brand guidelines. You re-explain the project background. You re-state your tone preferences. Projects eliminate this entirely — giving Claude persistent memory of your knowledge base, instructions, and conversation history across every chat in a work stream.

After this chapter you will be able to
  • Explain what Projects are and when to use them
  • Create a Project with a knowledge base and instructions
  • Understand how RAG scaling handles large knowledge bases
  • Share Projects with teammates using the right permission level

Core Concept — What Projects Give Claude

Use Projects When You Have
  • Reference materials you re-use repeatedly — meeting notes, brand guidelines, survey results, technical documentation
  • Consistent behavior requirements — always cite sources, always use formal tone, always follow our content template
  • Team collaboration needs — multiple people working from the same shared foundation and knowledge base
Visual — Three Steps to Set Up a Project
Step 01

Name and Configure

claude.ai/projects → "+ New Project." Descriptive name (e.g., "Q4 Product Launch"). Set visibility: private or shared with your org.

Step 02

Write Project Instructions

These apply to every chat in this project: what you're working on, tone preferences, specific rules (e.g., "always end with a CTA"), and process requirements.

Step 03

Build the Knowledge Base

Upload PDFs, DOCXs, CSVs, TXT, HTML — or connect Google Drive. Use descriptive file names: "Q4-Brand-Guidelines.pdf" not "doc1.pdf" — Claude uses file names to retrieve the right information.

RAG retrieves only the most relevant documents per question · Expands project capacity up to 10×

When your knowledge base grows large, Projects automatically switch to Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG). Instead of loading every document into memory simultaneously, Claude searches the knowledge base and retrieves only the most relevant content for each specific question. This expands usable project capacity by up to 10× while keeping responses accurate and focused.

Example — Projects in a Client Account Workflow

Real-World Scenario

A marketing agency managing multiple client accounts

Without Projects: every Monday the copywriter re-uploads each client's brand guidelines and re-explains their tone before drafting the week's content.

With Projects: each client has a dedicated Project. Brand guidelines, past campaigns, and approved content examples live in the knowledge base. Project Instructions specify the exact tone, format, and rules for that client. On Monday morning, the copywriter opens the correct Project and asks Claude to draft the week's content — Claude already knows everything it needs.

Prompt Example

Prompt · Inside a Client Project
Based on the brand guidelines, past campaigns, and client communication history in this project: Write a 3-email nurture sequence for our upcoming product launch. Target audience: mid-market SaaS companies (50–200 employees). Each email should take a different angle: - Email 1: Pain point (lead with the problem) - Email 2: Social proof (customer story or data) - Email 3: Direct offer (clear CTA, limited time) Under 200 words per email. End each with a CTA button text suggestion. Match the brand tone described in the guidelines.

What this prompt does:

Produces three differentiated, on-brand emails using knowledge base context — without re-uploading anything or re-explaining the brand.

Why this prompt works inside a Project:

  • Setting the stage: references "this project" — Claude draws from the knowledge base automatically
  • Defining the task: three specific email angles prevent any overlap or repetition
  • Specifying rules: word limit, CTA requirement, and brand tone reference are all explicit

Connectors

Connectors transform Claude from a standalone assistant into a collaborator that works inside your actual tools. Instead of copying data between apps, Claude reads your information directly from the source — and can take action on your behalf within those tools.

Core Concept — MCP and Two Connector Types

All Connectors are powered by MCP — the Model Context Protocol. Think of MCP as USB-C for AI: a universal standard that lets Claude connect to any application through a single, consistent interface. Any developer can build a Connector for any tool, and it works with Claude immediately upon installation.

Visual — Web Connectors vs. Desktop Extensions

Web Connectors

Cloud services · Set up via browser
  • Google Drive, Notion, Slack, Asana
  • Gmail, Linear, Stripe, Salesforce
  • Browse at claude.ai/directory
  • Desktop app not required

Desktop Extensions

Local tools · Desktop app required
  • Local file system access
  • Browser automation and control
  • Native app integrations (e.g., Figma)
  • Install via Settings → Extensions

Three Security Principles

Visual — How Research Mode Works: Four Steps
Step 01 — Plan

Claude Plans Its Research Strategy

Extended thinking activates automatically. Claude breaks down your question, identifies what information is needed, and maps out how to investigate different angles — before running a single search.

Step 02 — Investigate

Claude Conducts Multiple Searches

Instead of one query, Claude runs many — each building on what it learned from the last. It pursues promising leads and fills in gaps it discovers along the way.

Step 03 — Synthesize

Claude Assembles a Structured Report

After gathering from web sources and any connected integrations (Gmail, Google Drive, Calendar), Claude organizes all findings into a well-structured, readable report.

Every Claim Links to Its Source

Every finding is cited — so you can verify any claim, trace where it came from, and dig deeper on whatever matters most.

How Long Does Research Take?

Most reports complete in 5–15 minutes. Complex multi-source investigations may take up to 45 minutes — work that would normally require 2–4 hours of manual effort. Progress indicators show while Claude works in the background.

Becoming an AI Power User

Iterate, Don't Restart

The most common mistake is treating Claude's first response as a finished product. It is a starting draft. Review it, identify what is working and what is not, then give specific feedback: "Cut the first two paragraphs and make the recommendation section more actionable" is better than "make it better."

Evaluate Claude for Your Specific Workflows

Your work is unique. Running simple evaluations helps you understand where Claude adds the most value and where you need to provide more specific guidance:

  1. Gather examples — collect 5–10 samples of a task you do regularly (emails, reports, analyses)
  2. Create test prompts — write prompts using the Stage → Task → Rules framework for each sample
  3. Compare outputs — does Claude capture the key information? Is the tone right? What is missing?
  4. Refine your approach — adjust prompts, add examples to the Stage section, identify where human review is essential

Build Your Prompt Library

Every time you find a prompt that produces consistently excellent results, save it. Over time you will build a personal prompt library tuned to your role, voice, and recurring workflows. This library compounds in value the longer you maintain it — and it becomes one of the most transferable assets of your AI fluency.

Example — The Power User Difference

Real-World Scenario

Two product managers. Same tool. Different results.

PM A types "write a PRD for a new feature" and gets a generic template. They are frustrated because it doesn't match their company's format.

PM B opens their Projects folder where the company PRD template, last quarter's approved PRDs, and the product strategy doc already live. They type "Write a PRD for [feature] using the format in our PRD template. Align it with the strategic priorities in the Q4 strategy doc." They get a first draft that requires 20 minutes of editing, not 3 hours of writing from scratch.

The difference is not the tool. It is the setup, the context, and the specificity of the prompt.

Prompt Example

Prompt · Iterating on a Draft
This draft is good but needs adjustment. The tone is too formal for our audience (early-stage startup founders, not enterprise buyers). Please: 1. Rewrite the opening paragraph to feel more direct and peer-to-peer 2. Cut the second section by 40% — keep only the most critical points 3. Make the call-to-action in the closing more specific: tell the reader exactly what to do next and why it matters now Keep the data points and structure intact.

What this prompt does:

Transforms a good-but-not-quite-right draft into a polished, audience-specific final version through targeted, specific iteration.

Why this prompt works:

  • Setting the stage: identifies the specific gap (tone mismatch for the audience) so Claude understands the "why" behind the changes
  • Defining the task: three numbered actions with exact requirements — no ambiguity about what needs to change
  • Specifying rules: "keep the data points and structure intact" sets a clear constraint so Claude doesn't throw away the good parts
